CAI introduces a physics aligned framework for understanding instability in large language models. The concepts below extend CAI into prediction, topology, and longitudinal analysis. These ideas form a foundation for a new scientific direction in interpretability and reliability.

Compression Phase Shift (CPS)

CPS describes hallucinations as phase transitions. When a model carries too much compression pressure, it moves through three phases: Stable, Transitional, and Phase Shift. The final shift produces runaway instability similar to physical phase changes.

Why CPS matters

  • Predicts the moment a model becomes unstable
  • Turns hallucination into a measurable event
  • Supports real time early warning systems
  • Creates a physics based analogy for LLM state changes

Compression Strain Memory (CSM)

CSM states that compression strain accumulates over time inside the model. Repeated summarization, contradiction handling, or long context usage produces hidden fatigue. This tension does not disappear between answers but builds across the conversation.

Why CSM matters

  • Explains drift in long conversations
  • Measures strain inheritance across messages
  • Provides a fatigue style model for LLMs
  • Predicts future instability from past load

Semantic Pressure Gradients (SPG)

SPG shows that not all semantic regions carry equal compression pressure. Some regions are dense, overlapping, or bottlenecked, making them more prone to representational collapse.

Why SPG matters

  • Maps where hallucinations originate
  • Creates a spatial topology of instability
  • Supports visualization of pressure zones
  • Enables architectural improvements that reduce crowding
