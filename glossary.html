Glossary

Short, testable definitions. Each entry links to where it's formalized or used in practice.

Framework terms

Compression-Aware Intelligence (CAI)

A system design and evaluation framework that makes compression operations explicit, scores their cost with a measurable tension signal, and enforces evidence-based abstention when claims exceed safe thresholds.

CAI treats hallucination and contradiction not as errors to eliminate but as measurable signals of representation strain. The goal is not zero hallucination but explicit, auditable, governable compression.

Formalized in: Overview (Zenodo), The Theory, Proof

Compression Site

A discrete step in an information pipeline where data, context, or meaning is reduced. In a standard RAG pipeline: retrieval pruning, ranking, summarization, generation, redaction, post-edit review.

Each site is identified, scored with a CTS value, and linked to provenance. Naming the compression sites is Principle 1 of CAI.

See: Theory §3, Proof

Abstention Threshold (τ*)

The tunable gating value applied to the CTS path score. When CTS exceeds τ*, or when verifier entailment fails, the system abstains rather than asserting.

Abstentions should cluster where τ is highest. Uncalibrated abstention at low τ means the threshold is misconfigured.

See: Theory §6, For Engineers

Provenance

The complete record of sources, transformations, and parameters that produced a given output. A claim without provenance cannot be independently verified.

CAI systems attach provenance to every output so failures are auditable and review is fast.

See: Proof, For Engineers

Unsupported Claim

An output not entailed by available evidence or not covered by provenance. CAI systems abstain instead of asserting.

Reducing unsupported claim rate at fixed task accuracy is the primary outcome metric for a CAI pilot.

See: Proof, FAQ

Auditability

The fraction of pipeline steps reproducible by a third party using stored artifacts. Formally: $\text{Audit}(\mathcal{P}) = \frac{\text{steps with replayable artifacts}}{n}$.

CAI targets Audit ≥ 0.9 for public-facing claims and ≥ 0.7 for internal use.

See: Proof

Metrics

Compression Tension Score (CTS, τ)

A normalized per-site score estimating the risk cost of a single compression step. Combines four components:

$$\tau_s = w_1 \cdot L_s + w_2 \cdot D_s + w_3 \cdot P_s + w_4 \cdot U_s$$

  • $L_s$: information loss (e.g., KL divergence or task-accuracy drop)
  • $D_s$: task degradation
  • $P_s$: provenance gap
  • $U_s$: output uncertainty

Each term normalized to $[0,1]$ with weights summing to 1. Path score aggregates τ across all sites.

Formal definition: Theory §6

Contradiction Density (CD)

How often a model contradicts itself across semantically equivalent but differently-phrased prompts.

$$CD = \frac{\text{contradictions detected}}{\text{total outputs evaluated}}$$

High CD is a reliable proxy for high compression strain at the generation stage.

See: Theory §5

Contradiction Score (CS)

A measure of net conflict between two claims, derived from NLI entailment and contradiction probabilities:

$$CS(c_1, c_2) = p_{\text{contradiction}} - p_{\text{entailment}} \in [-1, 1]$$

Positive CS means net contradiction. Negative means entailment. CAI systems either reconcile or abstain when CS is positive.

See: Theory §5

Coherence Delta (CoD)

Loss of semantic coherence from input to output, measured by embedding similarity or entailment.

$$CoD = \text{Coherence}_{\text{before}} - \text{Coherence}_{\text{after}}$$

High CoD at a summarization step means the summarizer is losing more signal than it should.

See: Theory §6

Provenance Entropy

How dispersed or concentrated the evidence sources behind a claim are.

$$H_p = -\sum_i p_i \log p_i, \quad \text{Conf} = 1 - \frac{H_p}{\log K}$$

High confidence: a small set of strongly-entailed, verifiable sources dominate. High entropy: claim assembled from many weakly-supporting fragments. High entropy claims should trigger abstention.

See: Theory §6

Path Score

Aggregation of τ across all compression sites for a single request path. Used to compare pipeline designs, set compression budgets, and trigger abstention.

$$\tau_{\text{path}} = f(\tau_1, \tau_2, \ldots, \tau_n)$$

$f$ can be max, sum, or weighted combination depending on task sensitivity.

See: Theory §6

Tools

Hallucinet

A CAI tool that monitors model outputs for risky compression paths and triggers abstention when τ exceeds a configured threshold.

Hallucinet instruments compression sites and surfaces which specific sites are responsible for flagged outputs, making failure auditable rather than opaque.

See: For Engineers

MirrorNet

A CAI interface layer that makes compression choices visible to both users and the model. Where Hallucinet gates reactively, MirrorNet makes tradeoffs proactive and steerable. Users adjust evidence strictness and abstention level; the system shows how each adjustment changes its compression map.

See: For Engineers

Research concepts

Compression Phase Shift (CPS)

A research concept treating hallucination as a discrete phase transition rather than continuous degradation. Under rising compression pressure, a model moves through three regimes: stable (coherent, consistent), critical (elevated variance, sensitive to small input changes), and unstable (decoherent, contradictory).

The hypothesis: there is a sharp, measurable threshold between the critical and unstable regimes that can be monitored in real time.

See: The Theory, Proof

Compression Strain Memory (CSM)

A research concept modeling how compression strain accumulates across extended conversations and multi-step tasks. Each summarization, revision, or reinterpretation operation leaves residual strain, similar to fatigue in a material under repeated load.

Long-context drift may be better explained as accumulated CSM than as a context-length limitation.

See: The Theory

Semantic Pressure Gradients (SPG)

A research concept describing the uneven distribution of compression pressure across a model's semantic space. Some regions of the embedding or activation space are dense, overlapping, or poorly differentiated: regions where contradictions are more likely to be silently merged.

SPG aims to map these high-pressure zones and predict which prompts are structurally unstable before they are executed.

See: The Theory

Mirror Flags

Diagnostic markers that indicate disowned traits, false integration, or narrative breaks in inputs or outputs. They surface locations where compression bent meaning: where the system resolved a tension not by tracking it but by silently selecting one side.

See: Theory §5

Triarch

An orienting concept for CAI design: compression, decompression, and awareness held in dynamic tension. A well-designed compression-aware system doesn't only compress; it also decompresses selectively for verification and maintains awareness of the cost of each tradeoff.

Used as a compass for architectural decisions and design review.

See: About

Contradiction Template Atlas

A taxonomy of contradiction types that recur across domains. Templates include: factual contradictions (claim A and ¬A), temporal contradictions (different claims about when), scope contradictions (universal vs. qualified claims), and source contradictions (claims that cannot be simultaneously true given their provenance).

See: Theory §5

Benchmarks

Contradish

A CAI benchmark that evaluates whether a system can detect, surface, and reconcile conflicting information at production scale. Tracks three capabilities:

  • Detection: find contradictions in mixed sources
  • Tension: produce a τ distribution per site
  • Response: abstain or resolve with receipts

Contradish operationalizes CAI by turning contradiction handling into a measurable, comparable standard.

Site: contradish.com
